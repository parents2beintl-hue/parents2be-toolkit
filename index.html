Paternal PPD
Recovery Toolkit

A self-guided 90-day recovery toolkit for paternal postpartum depression. Built on the evidence base referenced across all four NHS perinatal mental health training modules. For fathers experiencing it and partners living beside it.

Before you begin: The NHS describes including fathers in perinatal mental health care as "aspirational within the current context of services." This toolkit exists because aspirational is not good enough when families are fracturing right now.
The only thing this toolkit asks of you is honesty. Not performance. Not the version of fine you show everyone else. The actual truth of where you are right now.

You don't have to answer every question today. Save your progress and come back. The only answer that won't help you is the one that performs fine when fine isn't true.

What you put in here is what you get back.

The other has the potential to be paid in time, in relationship, in the slow accumulation of distance that untreated paternal PPD can create in families. We know what that cost can look like. SECTION 05 OF 05

If It Gets
Worse

This section is not a failure state. It is preparation. Knowing what to do if things deteriorate is part of having a plan — not evidence that the plan isn't working.

Read this section carefully. If you find that you are already in the territory described below — stop reading and contact support now.
Thresholds requiring immediate action:

Thoughts of self-harm or not wanting to be here • Thoughts of harming the baby or anyone else • Complete inability to function • Significant alcohol or substance use escalating daily • Complete withdrawal from all contact for more than 48 hours

If any of these are present — stop. Contact support now.

Samaritans: 116 123 — free, 24/7, no judgement. You do not have to be suicidal to call.
NHS 111 — urgent mental health support
Your GP — call first thing. Use the word urgent.
A&E — if there is immediate risk to life

NHS guidance confirms: asking about thoughts of self-harm does not trigger someone to act on them. Telling someone is the right action.
Warning signs the plan needs adjusting: Your score would be higher now than when you started • You have not completed any week 1-2 actions after 4 weeks • GP not contacted after 3 weeks • Daily substance use to cope • Relationship deteriorating significantly.

If two or more apply — return to your GP. Show them this section. Tell them the plan is not working. That is not failure. That is accurate clinical reporting.
