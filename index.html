SECTION 01

Understanding Paternal PPD

Paternal postpartum depression is real, clinical, and measurable. It is not weakness. It is not selfishness. It is a recognised condition with a pattern of symptoms that present differently in men — which is exactly why it goes undetected for so long.

WHY IT LOOKS DIFFERENT IN MEN

Where women experiencing postnatal depression typically present with sadness and tearfulness, men are more likely to show irritability, aggression, withdrawal, risk-taking behaviour, and overwork. These symptoms are culturally read as personality problems — not illness. That misread is what keeps 80% of cases undiagnosed.

The Gotland Male Depression Scale — the tool underpinning this framework — was developed specifically because standard depression scales miss the male presentation entirely.

WHAT IS ACTUALLY HAPPENING — THE BIOLOGY

Research indicates that testosterone levels in new fathers can drop by up to 33% in the postpartum period (Gettler et al., 2011). Cortisol and prolactin shift simultaneously. Sleep deprivation compounds hormonal disruption. Identity undergoes rapid restructuring.

This is not a mindset problem. It is a physiological state responding to an extraordinary life change. It has a name. It has a mechanism. And it responds to structured support.

WHEN IT TYPICALLY EMERGES

Paternal PPD most commonly emerges between 3 and 6 months postpartum — later than maternal PPD, which peaks earlier. This timing means many fathers are dismissed as "just tired" during the window when support would be most effective.

Symptoms can persist for up to 12 months without intervention. Early recognition and structured support significantly improve outcomes — for the father, the partner, and the child's developmental environment.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR NEXT 90 DAYS

Recovery from paternal PPD is not linear. There will be better weeks and harder ones. The framework in Section 2 accounts for this — it is structured without being rigid, and it works alongside — not instead of — professional clinical support.

If you have not yet spoken to a GP, that is your first action. Not tomorrow. Today. Section 4 of this toolkit gives you the exact words to use.

SECTION 02

The 90-Day Framework

Not a programme. Not a challenge. A structured approach to the next three months — broken into four phases that reflect how paternal PPD actually moves, not how wellness content assumes it should.

PHASE 1 — WEEKS 1–3: ACKNOWLEDGE

WEEK 1
Name it. Use the language from Section 4 to tell one person — a GP, a partner, a trusted friend — what is happening. You do not need to explain everything. You need to say it out loud once.
WEEK 2
Reduce the pressure. Identify one thing this week that you are doing out of expectation rather than capacity. Remove it or defer it. This is not giving up. It is triage.
WEEK 3
GP appointment. If you have not booked one, book it this week. Use the script in Section 4. Bring your Gotland score if you have one. The framing matters — see Section 4.

PHASE 2 — WEEKS 4–6: STABILISE

WEEK 4
Sleep. Not "get more sleep" — an instruction that is useless to a new parent. Instead: identify your worst sleep disruption and address one variable. One. Not all of them.
WEEK 5
Physical baseline. Twenty minutes of movement — not exercise as performance, movement as regulation. Walking counts. This is neurological, not aspirational.
WEEK 6
Check-in. Complete the weekly tracker in Section 5. Compare your score to Week 1. You are looking for direction, not destination. Any downward movement matters.

PHASE 3 — WEEKS 7–10: BUILD

WEEKS 7–8
Identity work. Paternal PPD is partly a grief response — the loss of a previous self. Name one thing about your pre-father life that you miss without guilt. Then identify one small version of it that is still possible.
WEEKS 9–10
Connection. Isolation accelerates paternal PPD. One conversation with another father this fortnight — not about feelings if that is not where you are, but about anything real. The isolation breaks in the presence of other men.

PHASE 4 — WEEKS 11–13: SUSTAIN

WEEKS 11–13
Review and hold. Retake the Gotland assessment. Compare your score to Week 1. Share the result with your GP. This is not an endpoint — paternal PPD can resurge. The tracker and framework continue beyond 90 days for as long as you need them.
A NOTE ON NON-LINEAR RECOVERY

Week 7 may feel worse than Week 3. That is not failure — that is how recovery from depression actually moves. The framework is a structure to return to, not a ladder you fall off. If a phase is harder than expected, repeat it. There is no penalty for taking longer.

SECTION 03

The Partner Guide

This section is written for the partner watching it happen. You may have been the one who found this toolkit. You may have been living with something you could not name. What you have observed matters — partners are often the first and only people to notice.

WHAT YOU ARE LIKELY SEEING

Paternal PPD does not look like sadness. It looks like withdrawal, irritability, disengagement, overworking, drinking more, being short-tempered, or seeming absent even when he is physically present. It can look like he does not care. It is not that.

It is a man in clinical distress who does not have the language for what is happening to him, in a culture that has never given him permission to say so.

THE 7 SIGNS — WHAT TO WATCH FOR

01
Increased irritability or anger — not his usual temperament. Short fuse, disproportionate reactions, difficulty being around noise or demands.
02
Withdrawal from family — physically present but emotionally absent. Not engaging with the baby or with you in the way he used to.
03
Overworking or escapism — throwing himself into work, screens, exercise, or anything that keeps him away. This is avoidance, not ambition.
04
Changes in alcohol or substance use — drinking more than usual, or using something to wind down. Self-medication is a significant signal.
05
Sleep disruption beyond newborn exhaustion — unable to sleep even when given the opportunity. Or sleeping too much. Or waking with dread.
06
Loss of interest in things he used to enjoy — hobbies, friends, plans. A flatness where enthusiasm used to be.
07
Expressing hopelessness or feeling like a burden — statements like "you'd all be better without me" or "I don't know what's wrong with me" are clinical signals, not self-pity.
HOW TO APPROACH HIM — WHAT WORKS AND WHAT DOES NOT

What does not work: "You need to talk to someone." "Why won't you open up?" "I'm struggling too." These are all true. They are also likely to push him further in.

What works: Specifics. "I've noticed you seem really flat this week. I'm not asking you to fix it — I just want you to know I've seen it." Then leave space. Do not fill the silence.

The assessment: Showing him the Gotland scale and asking him to complete it removes the emotional ask. It gives him data, not feelings. Many men find this easier as an entry point. You can show him your score from the partner assessment and say: "This is what I observed. I want you to see it."

LOOKING AFTER YOURSELF

You cannot carry this alone. Being the person who notices, who holds it, who manages the household and the worry simultaneously — that has a cost. You are allowed to say that.

If you are struggling too, that is not weakness. It is the reality of postpartum life for both of you. The GP appointment he makes can also be one you make. You do not have to be well enough to hold everything together.

SECTION 04

GP Preparation

Most fathers who reach a GP appointment have already delayed for months. The conversation they dread is almost never as bad as they imagined. What makes the difference is framing. These scripts give you the language before you need it.

IF YOU ARE THE FATHER

USE THESE WORDS — VERBATIM IF NEEDED

"I have been struggling since the baby was born and I need to talk about my mental health. I have completed the Gotland Male Depression Scale — a validated screening tool for depression in men — and my score is [your score]. I would like to discuss what that means and what support is available."

That framing — mentioning a validated instrument and your score — changes how the appointment is received. It removes the vagueness that GPs struggle to act on. You have given them clinical language. They can work with that.

IF YOU ARE THE PARTNER

FOR BOOKING THE APPOINTMENT ON HIS BEHALF, OR ATTENDING WITH HIM

"My partner has been struggling significantly since our baby was born. I have completed a partner version of the Gotland Male Depression Scale based on what I have observed, and the score indicates probable depression. He has not sought help before now. I am here because I am concerned and I want to understand what support is available."

If he is not ready to attend, you can attend alone. A GP cannot discuss his case in detail without consent, but they can hear your concerns and advise you on how to support him and yourself.

WHAT TO EXPECT IN THE APPOINTMENT

Your GP may administer their own screening tool (often the PHQ-9). This does not invalidate the Gotland scale — it is additional data. You are entitled to mention that you have already completed a validated instrument and share your score.

The GP may offer talking therapy (CBT via IAPT), medication, or a referral to a perinatal mental health service. All of these are valid. None of them are permanent commitments. The first conversation is about establishing that there is something to address.

If you feel dismissed: You are entitled to a second opinion. You can say: "I would like this to be noted on my record and I would like to return in two weeks to review." That is not confrontational. It is advocating for yourself.

⚠ IF THERE IS IMMEDIATE RISK

If there are thoughts of self-harm or not wanting to be here — yours or his — do not wait for a GP appointment.

Samaritans: 116 123 — free, 24 hours, no judgement
NHS 111 — urgent mental health support
999 — if there is immediate danger

These numbers exist for exactly this moment.

HOW TO READ YOUR TRACKER

Your weekly total ranges from 3 (lowest) to 15 (highest). A consistent upward trend over 4–6 weeks is meaningful progress. A single bad week is not regression — it is data.
